# Music Therapy Collaborator Guide: AI-Based Music ISO-Therapy System

This guide outlines the goals of our project, how your expertise in music therapy is central to its success, and specific areas where you can contribute.

---

## 1. Project Goal
Our goal is to build an AI-driven, multi-agent conversational assistant that uses the clinical **ISO Principle** to guide users from acute stress or anxiety to a calm, relaxed state using traditional and modern Bengali music.

Rather than recommending random "happy" tracks to a stressed user (which causes emotional dissonance), the system will:
1.  **Match:** Play music that mirrors the user's initial emotional state (e.g., high-energy, tense music for a stressed user) so they feel "met" and validated.
2.  **Shift:** Gradually step down the energy and transition the mood of the music over a 3-to-4 song sequence, leading the user to a peaceful state.

---

## 2. How You Can Contribute

As a music therapy specialist, your clinical insights will shape the intelligence of the system. We need your help in three key areas:

### A. Music Dataset Curation (The Bengali Music Database)
The system matches tracks based on two dimensions: **Valence** (how pleasant/positive a song is) and **Arousal** (how energetic/activating a song is). We need you to curate and score a list of 15–20 Bengali songs (e.g., Rabindra Sangeet, modern acoustic, folk/Baul, band music).

*   **Task:** For each song, we need you to assign:
    *   **Valence Score (-1.0 to +1.0):** (e.g., sad/tense = negative, joyful/peaceful = positive).
    *   **Arousal Score (-1.0 to +1.0):** (e.g., slow/soft = negative, fast/rhythmic = positive).
    *   **Therapeutic Tag:** (e.g., "High Tension Match", "Soothing Transition", "Deep Calm Target").
    *   **Why this matters:** These scores form the database coordinates that the AI uses to calculate the therapeutic playlist trajectory.

### B. Questionnaire Refinement & Mood Mapping
We want to ask users a minimal, non-intrusive set of drop-down questions at the beginning of a session to establish their starting Valence and Arousal coordinates.

*   **Task:** Review and refine the question wording and answer choices.
    *   *Example Question:* "Right now, my energy level feels..." (Choices: Tense/Agitated, Energetic, Neutral, Relaxed, Tired).
    *   *Your Role:* Ensure the choices accurately capture emotional states and map them to the correct numeric values (e.g., does "Tense" correspond to Arousal $= +0.8$? Is "Tired" Arousal $= -0.8$ or should it be different?).

### C. System Evaluation & Testing
To prove the system is clinically sound before deploying it, we must validate the generated playlists.

*   **Task:** Help us design and execute the evaluation process:
    *   **Persona Creation:** Define 5–10 typical user profiles (e.g., "Sujata: experiences work-related panic and needs fast relief," or "Rahul: feeling sluggish and depressed, needs gentle uplifting").
    *   **Trajectory Review:** Review the playlists generated by the system for these personas. Do the song transitions feel smooth? Are they clinically appropriate? Does the progression actually follow the ISO principle?

## 3. Key Study & Reference Resources (Measuring Valence & Arousal)

To design our dropdown questionnaire and validate our emotional coordinate mapping, we draw from the three primary methods used in psychological research to measure Valence (Pleasure) and Arousal (Energy):

1.  **The Affect Grid (Russell, Weiss, & Mendelsohn, 1989) — *The Single-Item Grid Method***
    *   *How it works:* Participants are shown a single 9x9 grid where the horizontal axis represents Pleasure (displeasure to pleasure) and the vertical axis represents Arousal (sleepiness to high arousal). They mark a single "X" to record their state.
    *   *Clinical Value:* Proves that users can reliably report their current core affect in a single action without completing long surveys.
    *   *Reference:* Russell, J. A., Weiss, A., & Mendelsohn, G. A. (1989). *Affect grid: A single-item scale of pleasure and arousal.* Journal of Personality and Social Psychology, 57(3), 493.

2.  **The PAD Emotion State Scales (Mehrabian & Russell, 1974) — *The Verbal Adjective Method***
    *   *How it works:* Uses semantic differential items consisting of bipolar adjective pairs. Participants rate their state between word pairs like *Happy–Unhappy* (Valence) and *Excited–Calm* or *Stimulated–Relaxed* (Arousal).
    *   *Clinical Value:* Provides the exact verbal descriptors (e.g., sluggish, relaxed, tense) we use in our dropdown questionnaire to map to coordinates.
    *   *Reference:* Mehrabian, A., & Russell, J. A. (1974). *An approach to environmental psychology.* MIT Press. (See also: Mehrabian, A. (1996). *Manual for the Balanced Measure of Character, Temperament, and State (BMT).*).

3.  **The Self-Assessment Manikin (SAM) (Bradley & Lang, 1994) — *The Pictorial Method***
    *   *How it works:* A non-verbal pictorial scale where participants select from cartoon figures representing degrees of pleasure (from a smiling figure to a frowning one) and arousal (from a wide-awake, excited figure with an exploding chest to a sleepy, calm one).
    *   *Clinical Value:* Proves that visual/pictorial representations bypass language barriers and are highly intuitive for fast emotional self-reporting.
    *   *Reference:* Bradley, M. M., & Lang, P. J. (1994). *Measuring emotion: The Self-Assessment Manikin and the semantic differential.* Journal of Behavior Therapy and Experimental Psychiatry, 25(1), 49-59.
